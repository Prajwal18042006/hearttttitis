Treatment & Management

Treatment for heart disease is personalized. It usually includes lifestyle changes, medicines and, in some cases, procedures or surgery.

Lifestyle Changes

Healthy lifestyle habits are the foundation of heart disease treatment. They support your medicines and procedures and can improve your overall wellbeing.

  • Eating a heart-healthy diet
  • Being physically active as advised by your provider
  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Not smoking or using tobacco products
  • Limiting alcohol and managing stress

Medications

Medicines can help control symptoms, prevent complications and improve survival. Always take them exactly as prescribed.

Common Medication Types

  • Blood pressure medicines (such as ACE inhibitors or beta-blockers)
  • Cholesterol-lowering medicines (such as statins)
  • Blood thinners to reduce clot risk
  • Medicines to control heart rhythm
  • Medicines to reduce fluid buildup in heart failure

Tips for Safe Use

  • Keep an updated list of all medicines and doses.
  • Take medicines at the same time each day.
  • Never stop or change a dose without talking to your provider.
  • Report side effects promptly.
  • Ask if there are interactions with other medicines or supplements.

Procedures and Surgeries

Some people with heart disease need procedures or surgery if medicines and lifestyle changes are not enough. Your care team will explain the benefits and risks of each option.

  • Angioplasty and stent placement: Opens narrowed or blocked arteries.
  • Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery: Reroutes blood around blocked arteries.
  • Valve repair or replacement: Fixes or replaces damaged heart valves.
  • Implantable devices: Pacemakers or defibrillators help control rhythm or prevent sudden death.

After a procedure, cardiac rehabilitation programs can support recovery with supervised exercise, education and counseling.

Frequently Asked Questions

Many people with heart disease take long-term medicines. The exact plan depends on your condition and how your heart responds. Never stop a medication without talking to your provider.

Lifestyle changes are very important and sometimes enough for mild disease or risk factors. However, many people also need medicines or procedures. Your provider will recommend a plan for you.

Cardiac rehab is a structured program that includes monitored exercise, education and support. It is designed to help you recover and live healthier after a heart attack, surgery or diagnosis of heart disease.
