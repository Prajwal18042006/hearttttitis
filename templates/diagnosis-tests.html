Diagnosis & Tests

Healthcare providers use a combination of questions, exams and tests to understand how your heart is working and to diagnose heart disease.

Medical History and Physical Examination

The diagnostic process usually starts with a detailed conversation about your symptoms, health history, medications and family history of heart disease. Your provider will also perform a physical exam.

  • Listening to your heart and lungs with a stethoscope
  • Checking blood pressure and heart rate
  • Looking for swelling in your legs, ankles or abdomen
  • Reviewing other health conditions such as diabetes or kidney disease

Common Heart Tests

Different tests provide different information. Your provider will choose the tests that best match your symptoms and medical history.

Blood Tests

Blood tests can check cholesterol, blood sugar, kidney function and markers of heart damage. They help identify risk factors and complications.

Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG)

An ECG records the electrical activity of your heart. It can show heart rhythm problems, signs of a previous heart attack or other abnormalities.

Echocardiogram

An echocardiogram is an ultrasound of the heart. It shows how the heart chambers and valves move and how well the heart pumps blood.

Stress Test

A stress test looks at how your heart works during physical activity or medicine that makes the heart beat faster. It can help detect reduced blood flow.

Cardiac Imaging

Imaging tests such as CT scans, MRI or nuclear scans show detailed pictures of the heart and blood vessels to detect blockages or damage.

Cardiac Catheterization

In this procedure, a thin tube is guided through a blood vessel to the heart. Dye and X-rays help visualize blockages in the coronary arteries.

Preparing for Heart Tests

Your provider will tell you how to prepare for each test. This may include fasting before blood tests, wearing comfortable clothes for exercise tests or temporarily changing some medications. Always follow instructions carefully and ask questions if anything is unclear.

After testing, your provider will discuss the results with you and explain what they mean for your care plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

Most tests, such as ECGs, echocardiograms and blood tests, cause little or no discomfort. Some procedures, like cardiac catheterization, are more invasive but are done with medicine to keep you comfortable.

No single test provides all the information about your heart. Providers often combine results from several tests, your symptoms and exam findings to make an accurate diagnosis.

It depends on your age, medical conditions and risk factors. Many adults benefit from routine checkups every year or as advised by their provider. People with known heart disease may need more frequent monitoring.
