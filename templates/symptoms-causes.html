Symptoms & Causes

Heart disease can cause different symptoms—or sometimes none at all. Knowing common warning signs and causes can help you seek care earlier.

Common Symptoms of Heart Disease

Symptoms vary depending on the type of heart disease and may be mild, severe or even absent. Always treat new or worsening symptoms seriously, especially if they are sudden or intense.

Possible Symptoms

  • Chest pain, pressure, tightness or discomfort (angina)
  • Shortness of breath with activity or at rest
  • Pain, numbness or discomfort in the arms, neck, jaw, back or stomach
  • Rapid, pounding, fluttering or irregular heartbeat (palpitations)
  • Swelling in the legs, ankles, feet or abdomen
  • Unusual tiredness, weakness or fatigue
  • Lightheadedness, dizziness or fainting

Important: Chest pain or pressure, especially with shortness of breath, nausea, sweating or pain spreading to the arm or jaw, may be a sign of a heart attack. This is an emergency. Seek immediate medical help.

Major Causes and Risk Factors

Heart disease usually develops because of a combination of factors over time. Some can be controlled, while others cannot.

Risk Factors You Can Influence

  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • High cholesterol (especially LDL or “bad” cholesterol)
  • Smoking or tobacco use
  • Diabetes or prediabetes
  • Being overweight or living with obesity
  • Lack of regular physical activity
  • Unhealthy diet (high in salt, sugar and saturated fat)
  • Excessive alcohol use
  • Long-term stress or poor sleep

Risk Factors You Cannot Change

  • Older age
  • Family history of heart disease or early heart attack
  • Sex assigned at birth and hormonal changes (for example, after menopause)
  • Certain inherited conditions (such as familial high cholesterol)
  • Some congenital (from birth) heart problems

Even if you have risk factors you cannot control, improving the ones you can change still makes a meaningful difference.

How Lifestyle Affects Your Heart

Everyday choices—such as what you eat, how active you are and whether you smoke— can increase or decrease your risk of heart disease. Heart-healthy habits support normal blood pressure, cholesterol and blood sugar levels.

  • Balanced meals with fruits, vegetables, whole grains and lean proteins
  • Regular physical activity most days of the week
  • Not smoking and avoiding secondhand smoke
  • Managing stress in healthy ways
  • Getting enough quality sleep
